Sikh Gurus

The Sikh Gurus are the spiritual masters of Sikhism, who established the religion over the course of about two and a half centuries, beginning in 1469. The year 1469 marks the birth of "Guru Nanak Dev ji", the founder of Sikhism. He was succeeded by nine other human gurus until, in 1708, the Guruship was finally passed on by the tenth guru to the holy Sikh scripture, Guru Granth Sahib ji, which is now considered the living Guru by the followers of the Sikh faith. The guruship was also passed onto the Guru Panth, consisting of the Khalsa, however this means of guruship went into decline following to rise of "Maharaja Ranjit Singh ji".

Gurus Period Key Contributions
Guru Nanak Dev Ji 1469-1539 Founded Sikhism; emphasized equality, truth, and devotion to one God.
Guru Angad Dev Ji 1504-1552 Developed Gurmukhi script; compiled Guru Nanak's teachings.
Guru Amardas Ji 1479-1574 Institutionalized Langar (community kitchen); promoted women's equality
Guru Ramdas Ji 1534-1581 Founded the city of Amritsar; composed hymns for Sikh scripture.
Guru Arjan Dev Ji 1563-1606 Compiled the Adi Granth; built the Golden Temple.
Guru Hargobind Ji 1595-1644 Intoduced the concept of Miri-Piri (spiritual and temporal authority); built the Akal Takht.
Guru Har Rai Ji 1630-1661 Promoted environmental conservation and herbal medicine; maintained peace.
Guru Har Krishan Ji 1656-1664 Known as the "Child Guru"; served the poor and sick during a smallpox epidemic.
Guru Teg Bhadur Ji 1621-1675 Sacrificed his life for religious freedom; emphasized meditation and service.
Guru Gobind Singh Ji 1666-1708 Founded the Khalsa; declared Guru Granth Sahib as the eternal Guru of Sikhs.

Sikh Identity


The Khanda is a powerful symbol in Sikhism, representing the dual concept of Miri and Piri, which signifies the balance between spiritual and temporal authority. It is composed of three elements: the double-edged sword in the center (Khanda), symbolizing divine knowledge; the circle around it (Chakkar), representing unity and eternity; and the two swords on the sides (Kirpans), which stand for Miri and Piri. This emblem encapsulates Sikh values of justice, resilience, and harmony.
